Research Focus

My research investigates how climate variability shapes agricultural productivity and farmer welfare in developing economies. I focus on the interaction between environmental risk and institutional responses—particularly crop insurance—and examine whether formal risk-transfer mechanisms improve economic resilience under increasing climate stress.

Across my work, I integrate high-resolution climate datasets (ERA5, IMD) with district-level yield data and primary survey evidence to estimate heterogeneous impacts of temperature and rainfall shocks. Methodologically, I employ dynamic panel models, instrumental variable approaches, endogenous switching regression, and distribution-sensitive methods such as MMQR to identify causal relationships and quantify welfare effects.

Current Research Projects

Crop Insurance Adoption and Farmer Welfare under Climate Risk This project uses primary survey data from over 400 farmers combined with district-level climate exposure measures to evaluate the welfare implications of insurance participation. The empirical strategy addresses selection bias using instrumental variables and endogenous switching regression frameworks.
Nonlinear Climate–Yield Relationships in Indian Agriculture Using climate data spanning 1951–2020, this project estimates temperature and precipitation exposure functions and examines asymmetric yield responses under extreme heat and rainfall conditions. Distributional heterogeneity is explored using MMQR techniques.
Climate Risk, Institutional Design, and Adaptation This line of research studies how institutional access and farmer-level decision-making interact under environmental uncertainty, linking micro-level behavioral evidence with macro-level climate exposure patterns.
